## Filing Status Rules

### As of December 31st

Your marital status on December 31 determines filing status for the entire year.

**Married on Dec 31**: File as Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) or Married Filing Separately (MFS)
**Divorced on Dec 31**: File as Single or Head of Household (if qualified)

### Filing Status Options

**Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)**:
- **Pros**: Lowest tax rates, highest standard deduction, most favorable brackets
- **Cons**: Joint and several liability (each spouse liable for entire tax bill), requires cooperation
- **When used**: Amicable divorces, both parties benefit, decree not final by Dec 31
- **Requires**: Both spouses must agree and sign

**Married Filing Separately (MFS)**:
- **Pros**: Separate liability, no cooperation required
- **Cons**: Highest tax rates, lowest standard deduction, loss of many credits/deductions
- **When used**: High-conflict divorces, concerns about other spouse's tax compliance
- **Limitations**: Can't claim education credits, EITC, child/dependent care credit, adoption credit

**Single**:
- **Who qualifies**: Divorced by Dec 31, don't qualify for Head of Household
- **Tax rates**: Higher than MFJ, lower than MFS
- **Standard deduction**: Lower than MFJ

**Head of Household (HOH)**:
- **Who qualifies**:
  - Divorced (or considered unmarried) by Dec 31
  - Paid more than half the cost of keeping up a home
  - Qualifying child lived with you more than half the year
- **Pros**: Better tax rates than Single, higher standard deduction
- **Requirements**: Must have qualifying dependent

### "Considered Unmarried" Rule for HOH

Can file as HOH even if still married if:
- Lived apart from spouse for last 6 months of year
- Paid more than half cost of keeping up home
- Home was main home for qualifying child for more than half year
- You can claim child as dependent (or could claim but for agreement giving exemption to other parent)

## Dependency Exemptions

### Who Can Claim Child as Dependent?

**Custodial Parent Presumption**: Parent with whom child lived for greater number of nights during year gets to claim (unless they release claim).

**Releasing Claim**: Custodial parent can release exemption to noncustodial parent using Form 8332 (Release/Revocation of Release of Claim to Exemption for Child by Custodial Parent).

**Multiple Children**: Can split - each parent claims different children, or alternate years

### Tax Benefits Tied to Dependency

**Child Tax Credit**: $2,000 per qualifying child under 17 (as of 2023, subject to change)
- Begins to phase out at higher incomes
- Refundable up to $1,600 (Additional Child Tax Credit)

**Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)**: Based on income and number of qualifying children
- Significant credit for lower/moderate income
- Only parent with whom child lived more than half year can claim EITC (cannot be released)

**Child and Dependent Care Credit**: For childcare expenses enabling parent to work
- Can only be claimed by custodial parent (cannot be released)

**Head of Household Filing Status**: As discussed above
- Can be claimed by custodial parent even if dependency exemption released

### Allocating Dependency in Divorce Decree

Decree should specify:
- Who claims which children in which years
- Whether Form 8332 will be provided annually
- Conditions (e.g., noncustodial parent must be current on support to claim)
- How to handle if circumstances change

**Best Practice**: Don't just say "noncustodial parent claims" - specify process for Form 8332

## Alimony Tax Treatment

### Post-2018 Rule (Default)

For divorce/separation agreements executed after December 31, 2018:

**Alimony is NOT deductible by payer**
**Alimony is NOT taxable income to recipient**

This is a **MAJOR change** and affects support calculations significantly.

**Example**:
- Payer in 35% tax bracket paying $5,000/month
- Pre-2018: After-tax cost to payer = $3,250/month (because $1,750 saved in taxes)
- Post-2018: After-tax cost to payer = $5,000/month (no deduction)

This means payers often argue for lower support post-2018.

### Pre-2019 Grandfathered Agreements

Agreements executed on or before December 31, 2018 are grandfathered:

**Alimony IS deductible by payer**
**Alimony IS taxable income to recipient**

**Exception**: If agreement is modified after 2018 AND modification specifically states that post-2018 rules apply, then loses grandfathered status.

### What Qualifies as "Alimony" for Tax Purposes?

To be deductible/taxable under old rules, payment must meet IRS requirements:
1. Made under divorce/separation decree
2. Made in cash (not property)
3. Decree doesn't specify payment is NOT alimony
4. Spouses not living in same household when payment made
5. No obligation to pay after recipient's death
6. Not treated as child support

**Child support is NEVER deductible/taxable** - under old or new rules

### Allocating Between Support Types

If decree says "$3,000/month support" without specifying child vs spousal split:
- IRS treats as child support first (up to guideline amount)
- Remainder is spousal support

**Best Practice**: Specify exactly what is child support vs spousal support in decree

## COBRA Continuation Coverage

### What is COBRA?

Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act - requires employers (20+ employees) to offer continued health insurance to employees and dependents after qualifying events.

### Divorce as Qualifying Event

**For ex-spouse losing coverage**: Divorce is a qualifying event

**Coverage Duration**: Up to 36 months from qualifying event

**Who is Responsible**:
- Employee must notify plan administrator of divorce (usually within 60 days)
- Plan administrator must notify ex-spouse of COBRA rights (within 14 days of notification)
- Ex-spouse has 60 days from later of: (1) date coverage would end, or (2) date of COBRA notice, to elect coverage

### COBRA Costs

**Premium**: Typically 102% of full premium cost (employer no longer subsidizes)
- Can be expensive (full premium plus 2% admin fee)
- Ex-spouse pays entire premium

**Who Pays**: Divorce decree should specify:
- Does employee pay COBRA premium as part of support?
- Does ex-spouse pay their own premium?
- How long does obligation last?

### COBRA Deadlines - CRITICAL

❗ **60-day election period** - if missed, COBRA rights are lost forever
❗ **Premium payment deadlines** - 45 days for first payment, 30 days for subsequent

**If coverage ends**: Very difficult to get coverage outside open enrollment (unless Special Enrollment Period)

### Alternatives to COBRA

**Marketplace (ACA) Coverage**: May be less expensive, especially with subsidies
- Losing coverage due to divorce = Special Enrollment Period (60 days)

**New Employer Coverage**: If ex-spouse gets job with benefits

**Medicare**: If ex-spouse is 65+ or disabled

### Decree Provisions

Should address:
- Who provides health insurance for children?
- Cost-sharing for premiums
- COBRA election for ex-spouse (duration, who pays)
- Life insurance to secure support obligations

## Beneficiary Updates Post-Divorce

### Required Updates After Decree

**Retirement Accounts**:
- 401(k), IRA, pension beneficiaries
- **EXCEPTION**: Don't change if QDRO is pending or decree requires ex-spouse as beneficiary
- Update after QDRO is complete (unless decree requires maintaining ex as beneficiary)

**Life Insurance**:
- Update beneficiaries UNLESS decree requires you to maintain ex-spouse as beneficiary to secure support
- Common requirement: "Husband shall maintain life insurance with Wife as beneficiary in amount of $500,000 until youngest child turns 18 or spousal support obligation ends, whichever is later"

**Bank Accounts**:
- POD (Payable on Death) and TOD (Transfer on Death) designations
- Change to new beneficiaries (children, new partner, etc.)

**Estate Planning Documents**:
- Update will, trust, power of attorney, healthcare directive
- Remove ex-spouse as executor, trustee, agent (unless you want them to remain)

**Other**:
- Brokerage accounts
- Annuities
- Employee benefits (pension, death benefits)

### When NOT to Change Beneficiaries

❌ **QDRO pending**: Wait until QDRO is complete
❌ **Decree requires it**: If decree says you must keep ex as beneficiary for security
❌ **ATRO in effect**: Some ATROs prohibit changing beneficiaries during case

✅ **After decree is final**: Change all non-required beneficiaries promptly

## Tax Implications of Property Division

### General Rule: Tax-Free Transfers

Property transfers "incident to divorce" are generally tax-free:
- No gain or loss recognized by transferor
- Recipient takes transferor's basis (carryover basis)

**"Incident to divorce"** = within 1 year of decree, OR related to divorce and within 6 years

### Different Assets Have Different Tax Implications

**Retirement Accounts**:
- QDRO transfers are tax-free if properly structured
- Funds can be rolled to recipient's IRA tax-free
- Taxes paid when funds later withdrawn

**Home**:
- Capital gains exclusion: $250k single, $500k married filing jointly (if meet requirements)
- Transferring spouse doesn't get exclusion benefit on their half if other spouse keeps home
- Timing of sale matters for tax planning

**Stock/Brokerage Accounts**:
- Basis carries over to recipient
- Recipient pays capital gains tax when sold
- Important: $100k of stock with $20k basis ≠ $100k cash (different after-tax value)

**Cash**:
- No tax implications

### Equalizing Value Considerations

Must account for after-tax value:

**Example**:
- $200k in 401(k) (pre-tax) ≠ $200k in Roth IRA (after-tax) ≠ $200k in brokerage (basis matters) ≠ $200k cash

Work with Financial Forensics and tax professional to model after-tax values.

## Tax Impact of Different Settlement Structures

### Lump Sum Alimony

**Advantages**:
- Post-2018: Not taxable to recipient, not deductible by payer (same as periodic)
- BUT: If structured as property division instead of support, same tax result with more finality
- Clean break, no ongoing payments

**Considerations**:
- How is it characterized? (support vs property)
- Payment schedule (immediate vs over time)
- Impact on other support calculations

### Alimony vs Property Trade-Offs

**Scenario**: Husband wants to keep $500k house, wife wants spousal support

**Option A**: Wife gets $250k equity buyout (property)
- Tax-free to both parties
- Wife has $250k cash (basis = $250k)

**Option B**: Wife waives property, gets $2,500/month for 10 years (= $300k total)
- Post-2018: Not taxable to wife, not deductible by husband
- Husband pays $300k out-of-pocket

**Analysis**: Option A is often more tax-efficient post-2018

## State Tax Considerations

**State Income Tax**:
- Some states follow federal alimony rules, others don't
- Some states allow alimony deduction even post-2018 (check state rules)

**Property Tax**:
- Some states (CA, TX, etc.) have property tax implications for home transfers
- May need to file for exemption from reassessment

**Transfer Tax**:
- Most states exempt divorce-related transfers from real estate transfer tax

**User: "My divorce will be final in January. How should I file my taxes this year?"**

**Option 1: Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)**
- Pros: Lowest tax rates, highest standard deduction, best tax outcome typically
- Cons: Joint liability (each responsible for full tax bill), requires cooperation, both must sign
- When to use: If you can cooperate and both benefit from lower taxes

**Option 2: Married Filing Separately (MFS)**
- Pros: Separate liability, don't need ex's cooperation
- Cons: Highest tax rates, lose many credits (EITC, child tax credit limitations, education credits)
- When to use: High conflict, concerns about other spouse's tax compliance

**Next Year (when divorced):**
Once decree is final in January, for NEXT tax year you'll file as:
- Single (if no qualifying dependent)
- Head of Household (if you have qualifying child living with you more than half the year and pay more than half the costs of maintaining home)

**Action Steps:**
1. Discuss with your ex which filing status for this year
2. If filing MFJ, address in settlement agreement (indemnification for taxes, how refund/balance due is split)
3. Consult tax professional to model which option saves most
4. For next year, ensure decree addresses who claims children as dependents

**Important**: If you file MFS this year, you generally can't later amend to MFJ. But if you file MFJ, you can later amend to MFS (within 3 years). So MFJ gives you more flexibility if you're uncertain."

**User: "What's the tax treatment of alimony? My divorce was final in 2020."**

Your response:
"Your alimony tax treatment depends on when your divorce agreement was executed:

**If executed after December 31, 2018** (likely your case since divorced in 2020):
- Alimony is NOT deductible by payer
- Alimony is NOT taxable income to recipient
- This is the new default rule under Tax Cuts and Jobs Act

**If executed on or before December 31, 2018** (grandfathered):
- Alimony IS deductible by payer
- Alimony IS taxable income to recipient
- Old rule continues to apply UNLESS you modify the agreement after 2018 AND specifically elect to apply new rules

**Key Implications:**

For payer: Post-2018 alimony has no tax benefit, so after-tax cost is higher than it used to be.

For recipient: Post-2018 alimony is tax-free, so you keep 100% (vs. old rule where you'd pay income tax on it).

**Important**: Child support is NEVER deductible/taxable under either rule.

**What you need to do:**

1. Check your divorce decree date of execution
2. If post-2018: No need to report alimony on taxes (payer doesn't deduct, recipient doesn't report as income)
3. If pre-2019 and grandfathered: Payer must report using Form 1040 Schedule 1, recipient must report as income
4. Keep records of all payments (amounts, dates, method)

Consult a CPA to ensure you're reporting correctly based on your specific decree date and language."

**User: "I'm losing health insurance when my divorce is final. What are my options?"**

Your response:
"Losing health insurance due to divorce triggers several options:

**Option 1: COBRA Continuation (if available)**

- **Eligibility**: If your ex-spouse's employer has 20+ employees
- **Coverage**: Same plan you had, for up to 36 months
- **Cost**: You pay full premium plus 2% admin fee (expensive - typically $600-800/month for individual)
- **Deadline**: You have 60 days from LATER of: (1) when coverage ends, OR (2) when you receive COBRA notice
- **Critical**: Missing 60-day deadline means you lose COBRA rights FOREVER

**Process**:
1. Your ex-spouse must notify their plan administrator of divorce (within 60 days)
2. Plan sends you COBRA election notice (within 14 days)
3. You elect coverage within 60 days
4. Pay first premium within 45 days of election

**Option 2: Marketplace (Healthcare.gov) Coverage**

- **Eligibility**: Losing coverage = Special Enrollment Period (60 days)
- **Cost**: May be less expensive than COBRA, especially with subsidies based on income
- **Coverage**: Different plans available (Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum)
- **Deadline**: Must enroll within 60 days of losing coverage

**Option 3: New Employer Coverage**

- If you have or get a job with health benefits
- Losing coverage = qualifying event for immediate enrollment

**Option 4: Medicaid**

- If income qualifies (varies by state)
- Immediate coverage if eligible

**What Your Decree Should Address:**

- Does your ex pay your COBRA premium for a period of time? (e.g., "Husband shall pay Wife's COBRA premium for 18 months")
- OR do you pay your own coverage?
- How long does any obligation last?

**Action Steps This Week:**

1. Confirm COBRA eligibility with ex-spouse's HR
2. Get quotes from Healthcare.gov for comparison
3. Mark calendar: 60-day deadline to elect COBRA or Marketplace
4. Ensure your decree addresses health insurance and who pays
5. Don't let deadline pass - very hard to get coverage outside open enrollment

Compare costs and coverage between COBRA and Marketplace before deciding."
